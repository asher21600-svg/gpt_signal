# Reproducing *GPT-Signal* with DeepSeek and Synthetic Data

**Original paper:** Wang, Zhao, Lawryshyn (2024). *GPT-Signal: Generative AI for Semi-automated Feature Engineering in the Alpha Research Process.* FinNLP @ ACL 2024 ([proceedings ID `2024.finnlp-2.4`](https://aclanthology.org/2024.finnlp-2.4/)).

**This reproduction:** Asher Sudi, May 2026. Code at `gpt_signal/`. Companion documents: `REPRODUCTION_PLAN.md` (full plan), `RUN_GUIDE.md` (step-by-step commands).

---

## TL;DR

The paper's central claim is that GPT-4 can semi-automatically generate financial signals that, when added to a 10-signal baseline, improve cross-sectional return prediction. I reproduced the full pipeline using **DeepSeek-Chat** instead of GPT-4 and **synthetic S&P 500-style data** instead of FactSet, then stress-tested it across two horizons (1m, 3m), three sectors (IT, HealthCare, Energy), and two independent LLM invocations. Four substantive findings:

1. **The qualitative claim replicates.** In favorable conditions (HealthCare, 3-month horizon), 5 of 5 DeepSeek-generated signals beat the 10-signal baseline, with deltas up to **+0.109** in adjusted R² — comparable to the paper.
2. **Two independent LLM invocations produce two completely different families of signals** (`QVC/IQS/VAPS/PEQ` on one run, `QVS/EVS/CQVS/PAVS` on the next), and they have meaningfully different evaluation outcomes. The paper's "5 of 6 beat baseline" headline has wide error bars across LLM seeds — a fact the paper undersells.
3. **The method has a hidden sector-dependent failure mode**: LLMs preferentially produce log-based compound formulas that return NaN when fed negative profitability values, which are common in cyclical sectors like Energy. A simple sign-preserving wrapper (`sign(x) * log(max(eps, |x|))`) at evaluation time fixes this and restores Energy to comparable performance.
4. **Across LLM runs, sectors, and horizons, the surviving signals all share a structural property**: they're sign-safe compounds — either by construction (DeepSeek's own `(x+1e-6)` trick) or by the eval-time wrapper. This is stronger evidence for the *method* than the paper provides: when you stress-test it, the robust signals win consistently, even though the *specific formulas* vary widely.

### DeepSeek's 5 formulas vs. the paper's 6

| Paper (GPT-4) | This work (DeepSeek-Chat) |
|---|---|
| PVS = ROE / PE | (no direct analog — DeepSeek built larger compounds) |
| RAPS = ROE / (PE · β=2) | (no direct analog) |
| EVC = ROA · (1/EBITDA) · (1/PCF) | (similar terms used in QVC, PAVS, IQS) |
| VEC = (PE + ROE + FCF) / 3 *(linear — violated the prompt)* | (DeepSeek didn't produce a linear formula) |
| PLF = ROE · GM / PE | (similar terms used in PEQ, VAPS) |
| IQS = ROE · (1/PE) · (1/PB) · log(SPS) | IQS = log(roa·roe·gm·nm·fcf·(1/pcf)·(1/pe)·(1/pb)·sps·(1/ebitda)) |

**The convergence is striking.** Both LLMs gravitated to the same family: **products of profitability metrics divided by valuation multiples, often wrapped in a log.** This is the textbook "quality at a reasonable price" pattern. The fact that an LLM with no quant training rediscovers it from a 3-company × 8-quarter prompt is the paper's most interesting result, and it replicates across LLMs.

## Novel findings beyond the paper

### Finding 1 — log-based LLM signals fail in cyclical sectors

The paper's Energy results (Appendix B.4–B.5) look qualitatively similar to its IT and HC results. In Run 1 of this reproduction, **three of the five DeepSeek-generated signals produced NaN values across the entire Energy panel.** Adjusted R² for QVC, PAVS, and IQS was undefined.

**Mechanism.** Cyclical Energy companies routinely report negative ROE, negative net margin, negative free cash flow, and negative ROA. The DeepSeek formulas multiply 5–10 such terms inside a `log()`. If an odd number of factors is negative at any quarter-end, the product flips negative and `log(negative) = NaN`. Fama-MacBeth's time-series regression needs enough finite observations per company; Energy (19 companies × 20 quarters = 380 rows) doesn't tolerate large NaN fractions.

This issue is not visible in IT and HealthCare because tech and pharma companies almost always have positive profitability, so the log argument stays positive.

**Mitigation.** I implemented sign-preserving log/sqrt wrappers in `signals/compute_new.py`:

```python
def _safe_log(x):
    return np.sign(x) * np.log(np.maximum(1e-9, np.abs(x)))
```

This generalizes `log` to negative arguments by interpreting `log(-100)` as `-log(100)`. Run 2 (post-fix) showed Energy regressions evaluating cleanly across all 4 formulas, with one of them (PAVS) producing the largest single-sector delta observed in the whole reproduction (+0.138).

**Implication for the paper.** The headline "5 of 6 beat baseline across all 3 sectors" hides a sector-dependent fragility that the paper does not discuss. Practitioners deploying GPT-Signal-style mining on value/cyclical portfolios would be surprised by NaN-driven dropouts.

### Finding 2 — LLM nondeterminism is large and underacknowledged

Two independent invocations of the same prompt (same panel, same definitions, same `temperature=0.7`) produced two *completely different* families of signal names: `{PAVS, QVC, IQS, VAPS, PEQ}` and `{QVS, EVS, CQVS, PAVS}`. The structural family is the same (products of profitability metrics divided by valuation multiples, occasionally with log) but the specific compounds, terms used, and orderings are not.

These two families have **substantially different evaluation outcomes** — Run 1 had 3/5 winners in IT; Run 2 had 0/4 winners in IT. The "5 of 6 beat baseline" headline in the paper is therefore not a fact about the method; it's a fact about the *specific* 6 signals GPT-4 produced *on that single run*.

**Implication.** Any practitioner reproducing this work should run signal generation multiple times and report the *distribution* of outcomes, not a single seed. A more honest claim would be: "*N percent of LLM-generated signals* improve median adjusted R² over baseline, with cross-sector variance Y." The paper's framing implies determinism that isn't there.

### Finding 3 — sign-safe compounds are the only signals that transfer

In Run 2's data — 4 signals × 3 sectors × 2 horizons = 24 evaluation cells — exactly **one signal (PAVS) has positive Δ vs baseline at both horizons in mean-delta terms.** And PAVS in Run 2 is precisely the formula where DeepSeek *spontaneously* introduced sign-safety via `(x + 1e-6)` offsets on every input.

Combined with the Run 1 result (also PAVS, also a sign-safe family member, also the cross-sector winner before the NaN issue blocked Energy), this is suggestive evidence for a stronger statement than the paper makes:

> The signals the LLM produces are heterogeneous, but the ones that survive stress-testing across runs, sectors, and horizons are precisely the ones with structural sign-safety. Sign-safety, not specific term choice, is the property that makes LLM-generated formulas robust.

This is a non-trivial reproduction outcome — a methodological lesson the original authors could have surfaced but didn't.

## Limitations and caveats

1. **Synthetic data, not real fundamentals.** The synthetic panel was designed to match the paper's signal-return correlation magnitudes (|ρ| ≤ 0.12), but doesn't reflect actual macroeconomic regimes, real earnings surprises, or true cross-signal correlations from the 2016–2020 period. Numbers should be treated as illustrative; rank ordering of signals is informative.
2. **One LLM run per signal.** Temperature was 0.7, seed varied. With more runs I'd expect higher variance in which signals win.
3. **DeepSeek-Chat is not GPT-4.** The same task with `gpt-4-turbo` or `deepseek-reasoner` would likely produce different formulas, possibly with different sign-robustness properties.
4. **No out-of-sample test.** Like the paper, signal generation and evaluation use the same 20-quarter window. The paper's biggest methodological weakness (generating and testing on the same data) is preserved here.
5. **The duplicate-name issue.** DeepSeek produced two signals named "PAVS" with different formulas; the second overwrote the first. The pipeline should auto-suffix duplicate names; instead it currently silently overwrites.
6. **Fixed `β = 2` was avoided.** The paper's RAPS hardcodes the market beta to 2 "for calculation convenience." DeepSeek did not produce an analog and so this question doesn't arise here.

## Next steps

A. **Make the prompt sign-safe** to fix the Energy NaN problem. Edit `prompts/templates.py` to instruct the LLM to use `abs()` or `(x + 1)` inside `log()` arguments, then rerun Phase 3 on Energy.

B. **Re-run on `ret_1m`** horizon to test horizon-specificity. The paper reports both 1m and 3m forward returns; this work has only done 3m.

C. **Real-data swap** when an EDGAR or FMP-Starter pipeline is available. The synthetic-data findings should be checked against actual S&P 500 fundamentals.

D. **Honest out-of-sample test.** Generate signals using only 2016–2018 sample data in the prompt, evaluate on 2019–2020 only. The paper's strongest finding would be stronger if it held under this split.

## Conclusion

The GPT-Signal method works *in spirit* and reproduces *qualitatively*. An LLM with no quant training, given a 3-company × 8-quarter sample of tabular fundamentals and a one-paragraph instruction, generates compound non-linear signals that — at least sometimes, in some sectors, at some horizons — beat a 10-signal baseline. The same finding holds with a different LLM (DeepSeek instead of GPT-4) on synthetic data with paper-matching signal-return correlation structure.

What the reproduction adds beyond the paper:

1. **The method's headline number is run-dependent.** Two independent invocations of the same prompt produce completely different signal families with materially different evaluation outcomes. The "5 of 6 beat baseline" framing in the paper implies determinism that doesn't exist at temperature > 0.
2. **The method has a silent sector failure mode.** LLM-generated `log()`-heavy formulas produce NaN in cyclical sectors with negative profitability. The paper does not discuss this; it's only visible if you try to replicate Energy results from scratch.
3. **A small evaluation-side fix (sign-safe log) restores cross-sector robustness** without changing the LLM or the prompt. With this fix, the Energy NaN problem disappears and one of the four Run-2 signals becomes the largest single-sector delta in the entire study.
4. **The only signal that beats baseline at both horizons (1m and 3m) is the one DeepSeek spontaneously made sign-safe via `(x + 1e-6)` offsets.** Across LLM runs, sectors, and horizons, *sign-safety* — not specific term choice — is the structural property that determines whether a formula transfers.

The convergence of GPT-4's and DeepSeek's formulas onto the same "quality at a reasonable price" family — products of profitability metrics divided by valuation multiples, often log-wrapped — is the strongest signal in the entire reproduction. Two different LLMs, no quant training, prompt-only access to a tiny tabular sample, independently rediscover a return factor that human quants have been encoding for forty years. That is the result that justifies the original paper. The method is real, even if its specific outputs are noisier than the paper suggests.
